Options Greeks

The complete reference for Delta, Gamma, Theta, Vega, and higher-order Greeks — with intuition, formulas, and real trading examples.

Δ

Delta — Directional Sensitivity

Delta measures how much an option's price changes for every ₹1 move in the underlying. It is also the probability proxy for expiry in-the-money.

Call Range0.0 to +1.0
ATM call ≈ 0.50
Put Range−1.0 to 0.0
ATM put ≈ −0.50
ITM OptionDelta → ±1.0
Moves like the stock
OTM OptionDelta → 0.0
Lottery ticket behaviour
Δ(Call) = N(d₁)    Δ(Put) = N(d₁) − 1
where N() is the cumulative standard normal distribution

Trading Example — Nifty Iron Condor

You sell a Nifty 22,500 CE (Δ = +0.25) and a Nifty 21,500 PE (Δ = −0.25). Net portfolio delta = 0.00 — delta neutral. A 100-point Nifty move has no immediate P&L impact (only gamma starts creating directional exposure).

Key Insights

  • Delta is additive across a portfolio — sum all position deltas to get net directional exposure
  • A delta of 0.30 implies approximately 30% probability of expiring ITM (rough approximation)
  • Selling a delta-neutral straddle is not risk-free — gamma creates directional exposure as price moves
  • As expiry approaches, ITM options delta → ±1 and OTM options delta → 0, rapidly
  • For every rupee of underlying move, a portfolio's value changes by (Net Delta × Lot Size × ₹1)

Market makers delta hedge by buying/selling the underlying to stay delta-neutral. This dynamic hedging creates the "gamma scalping" phenomenon. When you sell options, market makers buy them and delta hedge — which is why large option open interest at a strike creates a "magnet" effect as expiry approaches.

Gamma — Rate of Delta Change

Gamma measures how much delta changes per ₹1 move in the underlying. It is the enemy of premium sellers near expiry and the friend of premium buyers.

Negative for short options

Spikes in final 48 hours
Seller's RiskNegative Gamma
Losses accelerate with moves
Buyer's EdgePositive Gamma
Gains accelerate with moves
Γ = N'(d₁) / (S × σ × √T)
where N'() is the standard normal PDF, S = spot, σ = IV, T = time to expiry

Why Gamma Kills Option Sellers at Expiry

On Monday, Bank Nifty is at 48,500. Your short 49,000 CE has Δ = 0.25 and Γ = 0.008. If BN moves to 49,000 (+500 points), delta becomes 0.25 + (0.008 × 500) = 0.65. Your short call is now deep ITM and losing money at the rate of ₹0.65 per rupee of BN movement — not ₹0.25. On Thursday (DTE 1), gamma of an ATM option can be 10–15× higher.

  • Gamma is the reason "max pain" at ATM strikes near expiry can produce violent last-hour moves
  • Long straddles have high positive gamma — they benefit from large moves in either direction
  • Iron Condors are "short gamma" — they need the underlying to stay still
  • The Decay Optimizer's "AVOID at DTE 1–2" rule is primarily a gamma management rule, not theta
  • Gamma risk is not linear — it accelerates. A 2× larger move creates a 4× larger gamma P&L impact

The Theta-Gamma Trade-off

Selling options gives you positive theta (you collect time decay daily) but negative gamma (you suffer when the underlying moves). This is the fundamental trade-off in options. Premium sellers collect steady theta rent but face gamma blowup risk. Premium buyers pay theta rent daily but profit explosively from large moves. The Volatility Engine helps you decide which side to be on based on current IV regime.

  • Theta decay follows a square root of time curve — the final 30% of time contains 50%+ of decay
  • Weekend theta: positions lose ~3 days of theta from Friday close to Monday open
  • Deep ITM and deep OTM options have very little theta — the most theta is always at ATM
  • An Iron Condor with ₹180 credit at DTE 4 earns roughly ₹25–₹45/day from theta
  • Theta is meaningless without considering gamma — always analyse the ratio (Theta/Gamma)

Vega — Volatility Sensitivity

Vega measures how much an option's price changes for every 1% change in implied volatility. It is the core driver of premium selling and buying decisions.

Long OptionsPositive Vega
Profit from IV expansion
Short OptionsNegative Vega
Profit from IV contraction
Highest AtATM + Longer DTE
Longer = more vega
Vol PremiumIV > HV on average
~2–3% historically
ν = S × N'(d₁) × √T
In practice: ν is quoted as ₹ change per 1% change in IV

Vega and the Volatility Risk Premium

Historically, implied volatility (IV) exceeds realised volatility (HV) by 2–4 percentage points on average. This "volatility risk premium" means short vega (selling options) has a positive expected return over time. The Volatility Engine's IV vs HV comparison directly measures this premium — when IV exceeds HV by 3+ points, vega sellers have a significant edge.

  • Iron Condors are short vega — they profit when IV falls after a spike (post-event IV crush)
  • Calendar spreads are long vega — they profit from IV expansion
  • Vega decreases as expiry approaches (weekly options have less vega than monthly)
  • A 5% IV drop on a position with −0.50 vega per lot generates ₹2,500 profit on a 50-lot position
  • Post-RBI and post-Budget are classic short-vega plays — IV crushes as uncertainty resolves

Rho — Interest Rate Sensitivity

Rho measures how much an option's price changes for every 1% change in the risk-free interest rate. The least discussed Greek, but relevant for longer-dated options.

Higher rates = higher calls
PutsNegative Rho
Higher rates = lower puts

India ContextRBI rate decisions
Repo rate changes
ρ(Call) = K × T × e^(−rT) × N(d₂)
ρ(Put) = −K × T × e^(−rT) × N(−d₂)
  • For weekly options (DTE 0–7), rho is so small it is essentially irrelevant in practice
  • Rho becomes significant for options with 3+ months to expiry (LEAPS, monthly far-dated)
  • When RBI raises rates: calls become slightly more expensive, puts become slightly cheaper
  • In the Indian context, the relevant rate is the 91-day T-bill yield (currently ~6.5%)

Second and third-order Greeks used by professional options market makers to manage complex positions.

  • Vanna — As IV falls after an event, deltas change even without the underlying moving. Market makers must delta hedge again due to vanna, creating additional buying/selling pressure
  • Charm — OTM option deltas decay toward zero as expiry approaches. Understanding charm helps explain why OTM options become worthless quickly in the final 2 days
  • Vomma — When IV is very low, vega itself is less sensitive. When IV is very high, small IV changes create large vega P&L. Vomma explains why long options bought at low IV are "cheap"
  • Speed — The rate at which gamma changes. Near expiry at ATM strikes, speed is extreme — small moves dramatically alter your gamma exposure
Σ

Portfolio Greeks

How individual option Greeks combine at the portfolio level — the foundation of risk management.

Portfolio Aggregation Rules

Portfolio Delta = Σ (Position Delta × Quantity × Lot Size) for all positions. The same applies to Gamma, Theta, and Vega. A diversified options portfolio that is delta-neutral but has large net vega is still exposed — a volatility spike will hurt it regardless of spot direction.

  • Portfolio delta tells you your directional exposure: +100 delta means you gain ₹100 per 1-point Nifty rise
  • Portfolio theta tells you how much you earn or lose per day from time decay across all positions
  • Portfolio vega tells you your sensitivity to an IV change: −500 vega means a 1% IV drop earns ₹500
  • The ratio of Theta/Vega tells you how much daily theta you earn per unit of vega risk taken
  • Risk limits: set maximum net delta, vega, and gamma limits as a percentage of account size
  • Correlation between underlyings matters — Nifty and Bank Nifty are 0.85+ correlated, so separate positions are not independent risks
